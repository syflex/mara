# Dutch A0 Syllabus

Scope document for **absolute beginner Dutch (A0 / pre-A1)**. This defines *what* an A0 learner must cover. Lessons are sequenced **from** this document, not the other way around.

A0 is a teaching convention — the CEFR officially begins at A1. In NT2 practice, "A0" / "pre-A1" means a literate adult with zero Dutch who needs the foundational sounds, vocabulary, and grammar before they can do anything A1 requires (short simple conversations on familiar topics).

**End-of-A0 success criteria.** A learner finishing this syllabus can:

- Introduce themselves (name, origin, residence, age, language)
- Greet politely (formal and informal)
- Count to 100 and tell time on the hour/half
- Say what they want, have, feel (basic needs)
- Recognize the alphabet and core sound patterns when spelled
- Ask the most basic questions (waar / wat / wie / hoe)
- Negate simple sentences (niet / geen)

A1 builds on this — short sustained conversation, multiple tenses, descriptions of routines, etc. (separate document).

## 1. Pronunciation

A0 priority: get the learner's ear and mouth working on sounds that don't exist in English. Without pronunciation grounding early, vocabulary recall is half-blind.

### 1.1 Alphabet

26 letters, Dutch names spoken roughly:
`a (ah), b (bay), c (say), d (day), e (ay), f (eff), g (chay — guttural!), h (hah), i (ee), j (yay), k (kah), l (ell), m (em), n (en), o (oh), p (pay), q (kew), r (err), s (ess), t (tay), u (ew — Dutch u, not English oo), v (vay), w (way), x (ix), y (Griekse y / i-grec), z (zet)`

- The Dutch **"ij"** is sometimes counted as a separate letter ("lange ij"), spelled IJ in capitals.
- Spelling out names is a frequent real-world need (telephoning, registration).

### 1.2 Consonants — the hard ones

| Sound | Examples | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **g / ch** | goed, lachen, acht | Guttural fricative. Northern Dutch ≈ harsh; Belgian / southern ≈ softer. The single most distinctive Dutch sound. |
| **sch** | school, schip, schaap | Initial: s + harsh ch. Final -sch is silent (mens-elij-k → menselijk; old: *historisch*). |
| **r** | rood, vier, broer | Varies by region: alveolar trill (rolled), uvular (French-like), or tapped. All are correct. |
| **w** | water, weten | Closer to English "v" than English "w" — bilabial/labiodental approximant. |
| **v / f** | vader / fout | Often blur — Dutch v can sound like f, especially at word starts. |

### 1.3 Vowels — short vs long

Dutch distinguishes short and long vowels by spelling:

| Short (one letter) | Long (doubled or before consonant + vowel) |
|---|---|
| **a** — man, kat | **aa** — maan, water |
| **e** — bed, hen | **ee** — been, weten |
| **i** — kind, dit | **ie** — kies, lied (long ee sound) |
| **o** — pot, kom | **oo** — boom, lopen |
| **u** — bus, dun | **uu** — muur, ruimte |

### 1.4 Vowel combinations (digraphs)

The non-negotiable A0 set:

| Spelling | Sound | Examples | English approximation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ij / ei** | same sound (two spellings) | mij, ijs / klein, mei | "eye" but tighter |
| **ui** | uniquely Dutch | huis, muis, tuin | no English equivalent — closest: "ow" in "now" said with rounded lips |
| **eu** | rounded | deur, neus, leuk | German *ö* |
| **oe** | long oo | boek, doen, moeder | English "oo" in "boot" |
| **ou / au** | same sound | koud, oud / blauw, jou | English "ow" in "now" |
| **aai / ooi / oei** | (less critical at A0) | draai, mooi, groei | longer diphthongs |
| **eeuw / ieuw / uw** | (less critical at A0) | leeuw, nieuw, ruw | |

### 1.5 Stress

- **Default: first syllable.** *VA-der, MOE-der, TA-fel.*
- **Common exceptions:** prefixes *ver-, be-, ge-, ont-, her-* are unstressed (*ver-STAAN, ge-LUK-kig*).
- **Reduced "e" (schwa):** unstressed -e and -en endings become "uh" — *lopen* sounds like *LOH-puhn*.
- **Dropped final -n:** in speech, "lopen" often → "lope" (especially in NL Dutch). Recognize but spell with -n.

### 1.6 Pronunciation milestones for A0

- [ ] Can distinguish *huis* / *muis* / *uit* (ui sound)
- [ ] Can produce *g* without saying English "g"
- [ ] Can read aloud short A0 words with correct vowel length
- [ ] Can spell their own name in Dutch letters

## 2. Vocabulary (A0)

Target: **~250–300 active words at end of A0.** Grouped by theme. Each entry: Dutch — English (notes).

**Anchoring strategy (two-tier).** A0 vocab is anchored against Bart de Pau's *1000 most common Dutch words* course (see [`RESOURCES_A0.md`](RESOURCES_A0.md)). His course is **topic-ordered, not frequency-ordered**: lessons 1–12 (= his "top 300") cover the **grammatical core** — pronouns, articles, numbers, days, clock, prepositions, question words, core verbs, basic adjectives. The **thematic A0 vocab** (family, body, house, colors, full food list) lives in lessons 15, 16, 20, 35. Each subsection below is tagged: `[Bart Lx, core]` = in top 300, `[Bart Lx, supplement]` = lesson 13+, `[mixed]` = some of each.

### 2.3 Cijfers (numbers) — ~30 items · `[Bart L7, core]`

**0–20** (irregular — must memorize each):
`nul, een, twee, drie, vier, vijf, zes, zeven, acht, negen, tien, elf, twaalf, dertien, veertien, vijftien, zestien, zeventien, achttien, negentien, twintig`

**Tens:** `tien, twintig, dertig, veertig, vijftig, zestig, zeventig, tachtig, negentig, honderd`

**Composition (21–99):** **reversed** — *eenentwintig* (1+20), *tweeëntwintig* (2+20). This is famously confusing for English speakers and must be drilled.

- `honderd, duizend, miljoen` — recognition only at A0

**Ordinals (recognition only at A0):** `eerste, tweede, derde, vierde, vijfde, zesde, zevende, achtste, negende, tiende, elfde, twaalfde`. Used for dates and floor numbers (*de eerste verdieping*, *3 maart*). Production is A1.

### 2.4 Tijd & datum (time & date) — ~30 words · `[Bart L10, L12, core]`

**Maanden:** januari, februari, maart, april, mei, juni, juli, augustus, september, oktober, november, december
**Seizoenen:** de lente, de zomer, de herfst, de winter
**Tijdwoorden:** vandaag, gisteren, morgen, eergisteren, overmorgen, nu, straks, later, vroeg, laat
**Dagdelen:** ochtend, middag, avond, nacht, vannacht, vanochtend, vanavond
**Klok (basic):**
- Hoe laat is het? — *What time is it?*
- Het is X uur — *It's X o'clock*
- half X — *X:30* (BUT: *half twee* = 1:30, not 2:30! Dutch counts toward the next hour)

## 3. Grammar (A0)

A0 grammar = the bare minimum to form a single comprehensible sentence and ask a single question. No tenses beyond present, no subordinate clauses.

### 3.1 Personal pronouns

| | Subject | Possessive |
|---|---|---|
| 1sg | ik | mijn |
| 2sg informal | jij / je | jouw / je |
| 2sg formal | u | uw |
| 3sg m | hij | zijn |
| 3sg f | zij / ze | haar |
| 3sg n | het | zijn |
| 1pl | wij / we | ons / onze |
| 2pl | jullie | jullie |
| 3pl | zij / ze | hun |

**A0 teaching decisions for pronouns:**

- **Default form is *je*, not *jij*.** All A0 example sentences, drills, and dialogues lead with *je* — that's how Dutch is actually spoken (*Hoe heet je? Waar woon je?*). *jij* is introduced **in the same lesson** as the stressed/emphatic form (*JIJ bent de winnaar*), but it's never the default form in templates.
- **Formal *u* is taught from A0, not deferred.** Every greeting/intro lesson shows both registers side by side (*Hoe heet je?* / *Hoe heet u?*). Rationale: the target exam (Inburgering A2) is formal-register heavy, and the most common real-world A0 contexts in NL (gemeente, dokter, supermarkt clerk, neighbor you've just met) are formal. In modern Dutch *u hebt* is fine (same form as *jij hebt*), so the conjugation cost is one extra row.
- **Stressed vs unstressed:** *je* / *jij*, *we* / *wij*, *ze* / *zij* — unstressed forms are default; stressed forms for emphasis.
- **When to use which register:** *je* with friends, family, kids, peers. *u* with strangers, older people, professionals, in formal correspondence. When in doubt, slightly formal is safer.

### 3.2 zijn (to be) — full conjugation

| | Present |
|---|---|
| ik | ben |
| jij / je | bent |
| u | bent |
| hij / zij / het | is |
| wij / we | zijn |
| jullie | zijn |
| zij / ze | zijn |

Example: *Ik ben Sam. Hij is moe. Wij zijn thuis.*

### 3.3 hebben (to have) — full conjugation

| | Present |
|---|---|
| ik | heb |
| jij / je | hebt |
| u | hebt / heeft |
| hij / zij / het | heeft |
| wij / we | hebben |
| jullie | hebben |
| zij / ze | hebben |

Example: *Ik heb een boek. Zij heeft honger. Wij hebben tijd.*

### 3.4 Articles: de / het

> **A0 rule (non-negotiable): always learn a noun with its article.** Never "tafel" — always "*de* tafel." Never "boek" — always "*het* boek." Article assignment is mostly arbitrary in Dutch (~75% *de* / ~25% *het*) and once a bare noun is memorized the article almost never sticks. This is the single most consequential A0 habit.

- **Two grammatical genders:** common (de) and neuter (het).
- **No reliable rule** — must memorize per noun.
- Useful tendencies (helpful, not exhaustive):
  - All plurals = *de*
  - All diminutives (-je, -tje) = *het*
  - Verb-derived nouns ending in -en = *het*
  - Most -ing, -heid, -schap, -teit, -tie nouns = *de*

### 3.5 Plurals

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| -en (most common) | boek → boeken, kind → kinderen (irreg. + -er-) |
| -s (after -el, -en, -er, -em + many loanwords) | tafel → tafels, computer → computers |
| -'s (after some vowels) | foto → foto's, baby → baby's |
| Irregular | ei → eieren, koe → koeien, stad → steden |

### 3.6 Indefinite article + negation: een / geen

- *een* = a / an (indefinite article)
- *geen* = no / not a (negates *een* + uncountables)
- *niet* = not (negates verbs, adjectives, definite nouns)

| Positive | Negative |
|---|---|
| Ik heb een hond. | Ik heb **geen** hond. |
| Ik drink koffie. | Ik drink **geen** koffie. |
| Het boek is mooi. | Het boek is **niet** mooi. |
| Ik werk. | Ik werk **niet**. |

Rule of thumb: *geen* replaces *een* or stands before unmarked nouns; *niet* covers everything else. Word order: *niet* goes toward the end of the clause.

### 3.7 Word order — A0 floor

A0 needs **declarative SVO** and **basic inversion for questions**. The full V2 rule and subordinate clauses are A1.

- **Statement:** Subject – Verb – (rest)
  *Ik drink koffie.*

- **Yes/no question:** swap verb to front
  *Drink ik koffie?* (Do I drink coffee?)
  *Ben jij Sam?* (Are you Sam?)

- **Wh-question:** question word + verb + subject + rest
  *Wat drink jij? Waar woon je?*

That's the entire A0 word order syllabus. Inversion after fronted adverbials ("Vandaag werk ik") is A1.

### 3.8 What's NOT in A0 (moved to A1)

- Regular present-tense verb conjugation pattern (stem + t / +en)
  *Some textbooks include this at A0; we move it to A1 entry so A0 stays anchored on zijn/hebben.*
- Past tense (any form)
- Modal verbs in full sentences (mogen, kunnen, willen, moeten as main verb) — A0 introduces lexically only
- Adjective inflection (*de mooie man* vs *een mooi huis*)
- V2 inversion after adverbials
- Subordinate clauses (omdat, dat, als)
- Separable verbs
- Diminutives as a productive pattern (recognize only)
- Reflexive verbs
- Relative clauses

## 4. Communicative functions (A0 can-do)

Adapted from CEFR pre-A1 / A1 entry descriptors, scoped to A0.

> **Register policy.** Every function below is practiced in **both *je* and *u* registers** where the situation calls for it. A learner should be able to do the *je* version with a peer and the *u* version with the dokter, gemeente, or a stranger. See §3.1 for the rationale.

A learner finishing A0 can:

**Identification & social contact**
- [ ] Greet appropriately formally and informally
- [ ] Say goodbye
- [ ] Give their name, spell it out loud
- [ ] State where they come from
- [ ] State where they live (city, country)
- [ ] State their age
- [ ] State which languages they speak
- [ ] Introduce a family member ("Dit is mijn …")

**Basic transactions**
- [ ] Say please, thank you, sorry
- [ ] Ask for repetition or slower speech
- [ ] Say they don't understand
- [ ] Say yes / no / I don't know
- [ ] Recognize their name when spelled aloud

**Numbers, time, basic info**
- [ ] Give a phone number
- [ ] Say how old they / a family member is
- [ ] Read a digital clock aloud (hour and half)
- [ ] Say today's day of the week and month
- [ ] Count items 0–100

**Wants and needs**
- [ ] Say they're hungry / thirsty / tired / sick / cold
- [ ] Say they want something (lexical, not full modal grammar)
- [ ] Say they (don't) have something

**Asking basic questions**
- [ ] Ask someone's name
- [ ] Ask where someone lives / comes from
- [ ] Ask how old someone is
- [ ] Ask the time
- [ ] Ask "what is this?" / "where is X?"

## 5. Scenarios (A0 — situations to rehearse)

Concrete situations where A0 vocabulary + grammar produce useful output. These map to lesson "spreken" and "mini-dialoog" sections.

1. **First meeting** — exchange name, where you're from, where you live
2. **Spelling your name** — to a receptionist or call center
3. **At the door** — saying hello, formal vs informal
4. **Counting at the market** — "X bananen graag, dank u wel"
5. **Phone call introduction** — "Hallo, met Sam"
6. **Family introductions** — "Dit is mijn broer / dochter / vriend"
7. **Reading a clock** — "Hoe laat is het?" — "Het is twee uur"
8. **Saying you don't understand** — "Sorry, ik begrijp het niet. Kan je dat herhalen?"
9. **Basic restaurant** — "Een koffie graag" / "Een glas water, alstublieft"
10. **Saying you're not feeling well** — "Ik ben ziek / moe / heb pijn"

## 6. The A0 → A1 boundary

When the learner can do **all** of the can-do items in §4 reliably and produces them without long pauses, they are ready for A1. Specifically, A1 begins where:

- **Vocabulary** moves from ~250 A0 words to ~600 A1 words; more abstract themes (work, education, routines, weather, opinions)
- **Grammar** introduces regular verb conjugation, modal verbs in full sentences, full V2 inversion, basic past tense (perfectum), adjective inflection
- **Functions** move from one-line transactional speech to two- to three-turn exchanges, simple opinions, simple narratives ("yesterday I…")
- **Comprehension** moves from isolated words/phrases to short, slow-paced spoken text on familiar themes

The A1 syllabus is a separate document.
